About Vijayawada

Kanaka Durga Temple

Kanaka Durga Temple is a Hindu Temple dedicated to Goddess Kanaka Durga. The deity in this temple is also popularly referred as Kanaka Durga. The temple is located in Vijayawada, Andhra Pradesh, India on the Indrakeeladri Hills on the banks of Krishna River.

Prakasam Barrage

Prakasam Barrage, one of the most iconic structures in Vijayawada, is a bridge that connects the districts of Krishna and Guntur. More than a kilometer long, it spans the Krishna River and offers splendid views of the lake.

Undavalli Caves, Vijayawada

A beautiful testimony to the rich history, culture, and heritage of the region, the Undavalli Caves is a set of rock-cut temples located amidst lush greenery. These caves, which are dedicated to Lord Padmanabha and Lord Narasimha, date back to more than 1300 years and were carved out of a single sandstone base.

Climate Change

Climate change refers to long-term shifts in temperatures and weather patterns. These shifts may be natural, such as through variations in the solar cycle. But since the 1800s, human activities have been the main driver of climate change, primarily due to burning fossil fuels like coal, oil and gas.

Climate change to make Vijayawada hotter in the next 50 year...

Climate change is likely to have a severe impact on Vijayawada in the next 50 years with the number of days a year over 35°C increasing from the present 95 days to 214 days. This in other words means Vijayawada will become warmer with a change in minimum and maximum temperatures.

A research projection on climate change in mega cities across the globe by National Geographic (NG) by 2070 reveals that Vijayawada will likely change from an arid, hot steppe zone to a tropical savanna zone. There will also be a marked changed in the number of rainy days.

"Vijayawada is in a tropical savanna climate zone marked by intense year-round heat The climate is nearly always warm and humid and average temperatures staying above 18°C every month of the year. Seasons are less defined here than in regions closer to the north or south poles. In tropical savannas summers are short, hot, and wet. Intense, tropical rains drop most of their water on the region during the summer, but the rest of the year is hot and relatively dry," the NG study said.

However, the NG study, which warned of a change in zone for Hyderabad by 2070, said though the climate conditions in Vijayawada will likely change, they are not enough to change its climate zone. It will likely change from an arid, hot steppe zone to a tropical savanna zone

"One way to get a sense of how Vijayawada might feel in the future is to compare it to somewhere that already experiences those climate conditions.

So, by the 2070s, Vijayawada will feel more like Galafi, a city in Djibouti in East Africa, does today, in terms of temperature and precipitation patterns and seasonality," the NG projection said.

Increase in Vehicle Population Pushing up Air Pollution Level in Vijayawada

Air pollution in the city is growing at an alarming rate due to increase in the number of vehicles. As per official records, the number of vehicles has increased by 15 per cent in the last year.

Green cover fast vanishing in city

Many trees have been felled in the name of development, complain residents Is Bezawada going the Hyderabad way in losing the green cover? Yes, feel many residents. Rising apartment culture, real estate boom and road widening works, the big and shade-providing trees are slowly but surely vanishing in different areas of the city.

Rise in Population

The current metro area population of Vijayawada in 2022 is 2,168,000, a 3.04% increase from 2021. The metro area population of Vijayawada in 2021 was 2,104,000, a 3.14% increase from 2020. The metro area population of Vijayawada in 2020 was 2,040,000, a 3.29% increase from 2019.

What should we need to do to control climate change

1.Put a price on carbon

Carbon pricing systems – such as emissions trading systems that cap emissions or carbon taxes that charge per ton – send a long-term signal to companies by creating an incentive to reduce polluting behaviors and to invest in cleaner energy choices and low-carbon innovation.

2.End fossil fuel subsidies

Fossil fuel subsidies send a different signal – one that can encourage waste and discourage low-carbon growth. By phasing out harmful fossil fuel subsidies, countries can reallocate their spending to where it is most needed and most effective, including proving targeted support for the poor.

3.Build low-carbon, resilient cities

There will be more infrastructure built in the next 20 years than in the past 6,000, the president told the audience. Cities are growing fast, particularly in the developing world. Just over half the global population is urban today; by 2050, cities are expected to hold two-thirds of the world population.

4.Increase energy efficiency and use of renewable energy

When we talk about energy, we have to talk about access. Worldwide, about 1.2 billion people lack access to electricity and 2.8 billion rely on solid fuels for cooking, such as wood, charcoal, and coal, which cause harmful indoor air pollution.

Through the Sustainable Energy for All initiative, the World Bank Group supports three goals for 2030: to achieve universal access to modern energy, double the rate of improvement in energy efficiency, and double the share of renewable energy in the global energy mix.

5.Implement climate-smart agriculture and nurture forest landscapes

The fifth area for action takes in both mitigation and adaptation. Climate-smart agriculture techniques help farmers increase their farms’ productivity and resilience to the impacts of climate change, such as droughts, while also creating carbon sinks that help reduce net emissions. Forests, too, are valuable carbon sinks that absorb carbon and store it in soils, trees, and foliage.
